Caipirinha

History:
Brazil's national cocktail began as a rural remedy rather than a pleasure drink. During the Spanish flu outbreak in Sao Paulo state around 1918, locals mixed cachaca with lime, garlic and honey as a folk treatment. The garlic and honey were eventually dropped, sugar and ice were added, and the medicine turned into a national institution. The name derives from 'caipira', roughly meaning country bumpkin.

Recipe:
Place lime and sugar into old fashioned glass and muddle (mash the two 2 tsp of Sugar, 1 of Lime, and 2 1/2 oz of Cachaca together using a muddler or a wooden spoon). Fill the glass with ice and add the Cachaça.

Fun fact:
Cachaca and rum are both made from sugarcane, but Brazilian law defines cachaca so specifically that it can only be produced in Brazil, within a narrow ABV range, from fresh-pressed cane juice. Brazil and the United States negotiated over the term for years before Washington formally recognised cachaca as a distinctly Brazilian product, meaning this drink required an actual trade agreement to be named correctly abroad.
