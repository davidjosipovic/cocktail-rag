Mojito

History:
The Mojito's ancestor is El Draque, a rough 16th-century Cuban remedy of aguardiente, lime, mint and sugar, named after the English privateer Francis Drake. Cuban locals drank it as much for medicine as for pleasure, since the lime fought scurvy and the mint made the harsh spirit tolerable. When smoother rum replaced aguardiente in the 19th century, the Mojito as we know it emerged, and Havana bars turned it into a national signature.

Recipe:
Use 2-3 oz of Light rum, Juice of 1 of Lime, 2 tsp of Sugar, 2-4 of Mint, and Soda water to taste. Muddle mint leaves with sugar and lime juice. Add a splash of soda water and fill the glass with cracked ice. Pour the rum and top with soda water. Garnish and serve with straw.

Fun fact:
La Bodeguita del Medio in Havana displays a scrawled note claiming Hemingway drank his Mojitos there. Historians have found no evidence he ever wrote it, and the handwriting doesn't match his. The bar has never taken it down, which is arguably the most Hemingway thing about the whole affair.
