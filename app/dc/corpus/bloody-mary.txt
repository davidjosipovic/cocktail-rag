Bloody Mary

History:
The Bloody Mary is usually credited to Fernand Petiot, who began mixing vodka and tomato juice at Harry's New York Bar in 1920s Paris, then brought it to the St. Regis in New York in the 1930s. The hotel found the name too vulgar and renamed it the Red Snapper. The public ignored this completely and kept calling it a Bloody Mary, and the hotel eventually gave up.

Recipe:
Stirring gently, pour the 1 1/2 oz of Vodka, 3 oz of Tomato juice, 1 dash of Lemon juice, 1/2 tsp of Worcestershire sauce, 2-3 drops of Tabasco sauce, and 1 wedge of Lime into highball glass. Garnish.

Fun fact:
The Bloody Mary is served on almost every commercial airline, and the reason is physiological: cabin pressure dulls sweet and salty taste but leaves umami intact, so tomato juice tastes noticeably better at 35,000 feet than it does on the ground. Airlines discovered this by accident and have quietly leaned on it ever since.
